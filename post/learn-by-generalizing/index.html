Learning by Generalizing and Exploring

2018-02-04
Life Math
1012 words 5 mins read

Contents

  • An example of the Generalization
    • Generalizing
    • Exploring

When learning, the following principles can be very useful:

  1. generalizing what we learn,
  2. exploring the concept we learn,
  3. naming things.

Generalizing-Exploring helps us learn more than what we are actually taught while naming help us recognize-apply what we learn in random occurances in our lives [1]. When we learn a new practical technique, the steps required to start incorporating it in our lives are:

  1. understand the technique/advice,
  2. make it a habit (the hard part).

In this post I will provide the technique to approach learning, and it will be the challenge for you to adopt it, if, of course, you believe in its validity.

An example of the Generalization

A friend of mine recently asked me to compute the sum $ J = \frac {1}{1 \times 2} + \frac {1}{2 \times 3} + \frac {1}{3 \times 4} + …$

To compute that infinite sum we can initially rewrite it in a more succinct form $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac {1}{n (n+1)}$

The solution of this problem is easily found if you notice that $\frac {1}{n(n+1)} \equiv \frac {A} {n} + \frac {B}{n+1}$ Where $A=1, B=-1$, and thus we have:

$$J =\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \left(\frac {1} {n} + \frac {-1}{n+1}\right) $$

One could see that terms cancel with the next term in the sequence and we are left with: $J=1$. [2]

Generalizing

To generalize that idea we have to pinpoint the precise structure that allowed us to perform that trick or the combination of tricks. In this case we took advantage of two major tricks, one was the decomposition and the other was cancellation. We will initially try to generalize the cancellation part. First of all we can agree that if $a_n = \frac {1} {n}$ then we have $J =\sum _{n=1}^{\infty} \left( a _n - a _{n+1} \right)$

We can then observe that the same could work similarly if we had $$ J_1 = \sum _{\text{consecutive integers}}(a _n - a _{n+1})$$

Or for some integer $i$ we could have [2] $$ J _2= \sum _{\text{consecutive integers}}(a _n - a _{n+i})$$

Where instead of the cancellation happening with the next term it would happen after $i$ terms. Can we further generalize this? Let’s consider the intermediate general case:

$$ J _3= \sum _{\text{consecutive integers}}( c_0 a _n - c_1 a _{n+i} - c_2 a _{n+i+j})$$

Where $(i,j)$ positive integers. One instance of those general series could be

$$ J _3'= \sum _{\text{consecutive integers}}( a _n - 9 a _{n+2} + 3 a _{n+5})$$

Where $(i=2,j=3)$. Of course this particular series might not have the cancellation property that we aspire to. To find out under which constraints there will be a cancellation (if there will be a cancellation at all) consider the following figure, where each row represents one term of the series, starting from $(i=1)$ and ending in a finite row-term $(i=k+i+j)$

We can see that after a certain row, if there are sufficiently many rows, $a(1+i+j)$ appear in each column only once. The same is true for $a(k+i+j)$ and all the intermediary terms. Therefore those terms will have a coefficient of $(c_0+c_1+c_2)$ in the final sum, and for them to be zero we simply require that $c_0+c_1+c_2 = 0$

Exploring

After writing that sum in this form, one could observe that the product of consecutive integers is “visually” similar to the left hand side of

$$ \frac {n(n+1)}{2} = \sum_{k=1}^{n} k $$

But they are not quite the same. That’s not a major problem though, since we can simply rewrite this as

$$ n(n+1) = 2 \sum _{k=1}^{n} k \Rightarrow \frac {1}{ n(n+1) } = 0.5 \frac {1 } {\sum _{k=1}^{n} k}$$

Using this derived result in the original series, we get $ J = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac {1}{n (n+1)} = 0.5 \sum _{n=1}^{\infty} \frac 1 {\sum _{k=1}^{n} k} $

By exploring this path we 1) have fun, 2) get more familiar with manipulating series, and also 3) capture the relation of this special-looking series in our memory with the initial sum $J$. Even making the thought of the existance of a correspondence between $J$ and the weird looking inverse sum might help us depending on whether that formula has a structure that emerges in many cases.

Of course we are humans, and unfortunately that practice will possibly not scale very well if abused. It is important to explore around your field of interest or around critical concepts to avoid getting lost. For instance let’s say that your field of interest is Graph Theory. In that case you would want to attempt to correlate the initial series with a graph-theoretic result. For instance the formula $$\frac {n(n-1)}{2}$$ describes the number of edges, $|E_n|$, in a complete graph $K_n$ with $n$-nodes. Thus

$$ \frac 1 {n(n+1)} = \frac {0.5} {|E_{n+1}|}$$

Finally

$$2J = \sum _{n=1}^{\infty} \frac 1 {|E _{n+1}|} $$

Now you should ask yourself: Can I describe the final expression in simple words relating to graphs? The more precise, specific, and simple the wording you will employ, the better the result you have. In this case I would say that we have an expression that relates inversely the edges of complete graphs $K_n$ with the series.

If your field of interest is Electric Circuits, you might find useful to relate this formula with $$\frac 1 {R_{total}} = \sum _{i=1} ^{k} \frac 1 {R_i}$$ Considering each term in the series as a parallel resistance. You might think that this is not terribly useful, and indeed it might not be, but its not unheard of to have such decompositions in stunningly “out of the box” proofs.

It is important to being just the right amount of general of course - being very general can make things either hard to recognize or not very useful in practice.
