Predictability of human actions in deterministic systems

4 February 2018·6 mins·

Have you ever wondered whether our actions and decisions are truly our own, or are they merely the result of a complex web of factors beyond our control? The question of determinism has intrigued philosophers, scientists, and thinkers for centuries. In this blog post, we will merely dip our feet into the fascinating world of determinism and its potential implications on the predictability of human actions. We assume that the world is deterministic for the sake of our discussion.

Modeling the task and the Butterfly effect

In a deterministic world, the exact current state of the world (denoted as $World(t)$ at time $t$) would determine everything that is destined to happen, making it seem as though we have no control over the outcome. However, we often feel like we have a choice, as if we are in charge of our decisions, and that these could lead to different results. Many have posited that this feeling of control might just be an illusion. In such a deterministic world, our actions would be the result of stimuli we encounter, as well as our own state, over which we have no control since it already belongs in the past. In that case, we would have:

$$World(t+1)=\mathcal F(World(t))$$

Here, $\mathcal F$ represents a deterministic function, and we have assumed that time is quantized. Naturally, this function would be incredibly complex and chaotic. To reduce the complexity of the function and its negligible dependence on myriads of factors, such as predicting the impact of the wind in the Sahara 10 years ago on our lives today, we need to introduce the notion of randomness, or noise, for hard-to-measure and small-scale phenomena. We can then arrive at a new, much simpler, function $f$ that depends on the current state of the world as well as a random variable, $\epsilon$ (noise), which captures the influence of non-modeled phenomena:

$$World(t+1)=f(World(t),\epsilon)$$

Note that, although not visible in the above formulation, $f$ need not depend on all the parameters of $World(t)$. The more accurate we make $f$, the more complicated it will tend to be, but on the positive side, we will have smaller variability of the now random $World(t+1)$. We write $World(t+1)\equiv World_\epsilon(t+1)$ to explicitly express the dependence on the noise.

Assuming we know the relevant parts of $World(t)$ affecting $f$ (in other words, conditional on the current situation as far as $f$ is concerned), to have any hope for predictability, we would want $f$ to be accurate enough such that we have a guarantee, that two independent runs of the current world (with corresponding noise values $\epsilon_1,\epsilon_2$) are not going to vary significantly with high probability. Mathematically, for a small $\delta$, this can be written as:

$$\Pr( World_{\epsilon_1}(t+1) \approx World_{\epsilon_2}(t+1)) > 1- \delta$$

Here, we assumed some norm of similarity between worlds. The butterfly effect, in this context, would suggest that with high probability, the two worlds will eventually diverge, for most choices of $f$:

$$\Pr( World_{\epsilon_1}(t+k) \approx World_{\epsilon_2}(t+k)) < \delta$$

Does this mean that predictions are inevitable, even in a determinsitic world? This seems counterintuitive. Indeed, when we focus on specific aspects of the world, such as the reactions of individuals, we can find patterns and regularities. In other words, we are not aiming to predict the entirety of the world, but rather a relevant and significant projection, denoted as $\Pi(World(\cdots))$. We can examine various examples to demonstrate that human reactions can be more predictable and adhere to statistical regularities, rather than being purely chaotic.

An analogy about the distribution of human behavior.

Humans are striving to optimize their lives with respect to some objective, weather it be feeling of importance, wealth or comfort or some combination of similar factors. Regardless, more or less, objective functions follow patterns themselves. Suppose monetary wealth is the objective of each human for simplicity. There are many ways one can try to optimize such an objective and therefore the range of human behaviors will broadly vary. However, through imitation and experience we are refining the “policy” of our behavior in order to heuristically optimize such objective. If ecah policy was visualized as a two dimensional point, we would find clustering and patterns with humans chasing the ideal policy which is moving as the various other dynamics shift. All behaviors could then be traced back to this hidden-simplicity as the game of life of Conman gives rise to trumendous complexity from a few simple dynamics.

Regularities, and the Chaotic versus the Predictaable

Suppose you are in a big conference with a lot of impressive scientists. Everyone has a different favorite scientist, and they march towards them from a random initial position, with common speed (following one, followed by one). A pattern will eventually emerge, dependent on the exact locations, with almost always one common characteristic: a loop emerging. Even though this is a fairly complicated system, still, this pattern always emerges. Yet, its hard to tell how it would look like from the beginning. In fact, if you just swap the following/follower list for 2 random pairs, very often you will find a very different final arrangement. Test this yourself. In the following simulation, at the top you see one world, and in the other at the bottom, you have a world where 2 pairs are swapped initially compared to the world at the top. You can modify the number of pairs swapped too. Try the restart button. Between the two worlds, the mean position of the top and bottom are compared to see how much these differ. The mean, as an averaging operator, tends to smooth things out and often has better predictability properties. Note that this mean is in fact mostly ranging around the center of the area.

Examples of Predictability

Example 1: Predictable Reactions in Dementia Patients

My grandmother, who suffers from dementia, frequently visits my room. There are two possible scenarios when she enters: either I am present or absent. An unframed picture of me as a child rests on my bookcase. Since dementia patients often struggle to form new memories, it’s reasonable to assume that her mental state falls into one of a few categories each time she visits my room. Moreover, the stimuli she encounters remain relatively consistent. In many instances, her reaction follows a predictable pattern: she notices the picture, picks it up, and becomes filled with joy.

Example 2: Recalling Thoughts through Familiar Stimuli

On occasion, I find myself contemplating something while browsing through a scrolling application, only to lose track of the thought moments later. Surprisingly, recovering the lost thought is often as simple as re-exposing myself to the same stimulus. In other words, retracing my steps in the scrolling application often triggers the thought to resurface, illustrating the predictability of human thoughts in response to consistent stimuli.
